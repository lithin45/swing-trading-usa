# News & Sentiment Signals for a Swing-Trading Signal System (US Equities)

## Overview

For a signal-only, percentage-rule swing system holding days to a month, the single most exploitable, well-documented edge in this entire space is **post-earnings-announcement drift (PEAD)** and its close cousin **analyst-revision momentum** — both are slow, persistent underreactions to *hard*, verifiable news that drift in the direction of the surprise for weeks. Almost everything else — especially raw social-media sentiment polarity — is mostly noise that predicts volume and volatility far better than it predicts returns, with a few narrow, conditional exceptions (sentiment *combined with* abnormal message-volume spikes).

This report covers (1) how news types move stocks on the swing horizon, (2) a cost-annotated catalog of news/sentiment data sources, (3) NLP scoring methods, (4) what social sentiment is signal vs. noise, (5) decay/timing, and (6) how to turn it all into a computable 0–100 sub-score. The tone throughout is deliberately skeptical: most published "X predicts the market" results are fragile, in-sample, or have decayed since publication.

Key framing facts:
- PEAD is "one of the most replicated anomalies in all of empirical finance" (Bernard & Thomas 1989/1990). Bernard & Thomas (1990) zero-investment SUE-decile portfolios earned roughly **8–9% per quarter (~35% annualized) before transaction costs** at the anomaly's peak — but the edge has since eroded sharply. Charles Martineau (2022, "Rest in Peace Post-Earnings Announcement Drift") shows PEAD **began disappearing from non-microcap stocks around 2001 and was essentially zero for large-cap stocks by 2006**. The surviving edge lives almost entirely in small/illiquid, low-coverage names.
- Heston & Sinha (2017, *Financial Analysts Journal* 73(3):67–83), using >900,000 stories scored by a proprietary Thomson-Reuters neural net: **"daily news predicts stock returns for only 1 to 2 days... Weekly news, however, predicts stock returns for one quarter. Positive news stories increase stock returns quickly, but negative stories have a long delayed reaction."**
- Tetlock (2007, *Journal of Finance* 62:1139–1168), using a daily *Wall Street Journal* column: **"high media pessimism predicts downward pressure on market prices followed by a reversion to fundamentals, and unusually high or low pessimism predicts high market trading volume... consistent with theoretical models of noise and liquidity traders."** In other words, much "sentiment" is temporary noise-trader pressure, not information.

## News signal types (what actually moves stocks on a swing horizon)

**1. Earnings surprises (PEAD) — the strongest swing signal.** Bernard & Thomas (1989, *Journal of Accounting Research* 27:1–36), using 1974–1985 data, found the SUE top-minus-bottom-decile return spread was **"positive in 41 of the 48 quarters from 1974 to 1985 and in 11 of the 16 quarters in which returns on the NYSE index are negative."** Drift continues for at least 60 days and a disproportionate ~25–30% of total drift concentrates in the 3-day windows around the *next* earnings announcement (a structural autocorrelation pattern: seasonal-earnings autocorrelations ~0.34, 0.19, 0.06 at lags 1–3). Practitioner implementations long the top SUE / earnings-announcement-return (EAR) quintile and hold ~60 trading days. **Tradeable**, but the magnitude has declined sharply over time and is now concentrated in less-liquid, lower-coverage, smaller-cap names; "beats" can still fall on weak guidance.

**2. Analyst estimate revisions (revision momentum) — strong and complementary.** Stickel (1991) and Gleason & Lee (2003, *The Accounting Review*) document post-revision price drift over the following weeks-to-months. Crucially, Gleason & Lee show the drift is *larger* for (a) "high-innovation" revisions that move away from consensus rather than herd toward it, (b) low-coverage firms, and (c) obscure-but-accurate (non-"celebrity") analysts — and that a chunk of the delayed response corrects around the next earnings event. **Tradeable**, and revision direction/breadth is a clean computable input. Practitioner framing (John Bogle Jr.): "Share-price momentum results from earnings-estimate momentum."

**3. Analyst rating changes (upgrades/downgrades/initiations/price-target changes).** These move stocks within minutes and the initial reaction is largely immediate. The persistent-edge nuances: cluster upgrades (multiple firms within a short window) act as a momentum signal; magnitude of the price-target change matters more than the rating notch (a $100→$160 target implies far more conviction than $100→$105); downgrades tend to produce sharper, faster reactions than upgrades. Wall Street has a structural bullish bias (few "Sell" ratings; "Hold" is a soft sell). Much of a single upgrade is priced in fast — treat single rating changes as weaker and faster-decaying than estimate revisions.

**4. M&A news.** Target stock typically gaps to just below the offer — the NY Fed's merger-options analysis reports the **median target jumps ~27% on announcement and then trades at a ~3.5% arbitrage spread to the offer**. The initial gap is immediate; the remaining spread is compensation for deal risk, not free drift. A 2008–2021 study of 340 US deals found target returns stay positive over 30 days with much of the move occurring several days after announcement (underreaction consistent with limited attention), but downside is severe if the deal breaks (negatively skewed). Historically ~89–90% of announced deals close; Verdad's 835-deal study (2000–2020) found arbitrageurs earned ~2.0% on successful deals and lost ~2.8% on cancelled ones (blended ~1.5% on long exposure). Tradeable mainly as long-the-target with deal-risk awareness.

**5. FDA/regulatory & clinical-trial news (biotech).** Binary, high-variance catalysts: small-cap single-asset biotechs can see 50–200% single-day moves on approval/failure. Event-study evidence (13,807 trials, 2000–2020) shows **Phase 3 and Phase 2/3 readouts produce the largest abnormal returns** (Phase 2/3 ~2.3–3.1% higher day-0/0–1 abnormal returns than Phase 1/2); markets *overreact* to small-firm failures and *underreact* to large-firm failures (post-event drift). "Sell-the-news" is common when approval was priced in. There is a documented pre-announcement leakage/insider-trading signature (Phase III "winners" rose +27% vs "losers" −4% from 120 to 3 days *before* public announcement). Tradeable only with strict position sizing; not a clean systematic signal for a small account.

**6. Breaking news / headlines generally.** Chan (2003) "drift and reversal after headlines": stocks drift after public news but *reverse* after price moves with no news. Tetlock (2011) "stale news": reactions to recycled/stale stories reverse within a week, while reactions to genuinely new information persist or continue — **novelty matters enormously**. Positive news is incorporated fast; negative news gets a long-delayed reaction, much of it around the next earnings date (Heston & Sinha 2017).

## Sentiment sources (with cost/access notes)

Prioritized for a small account: start with the free tiers and SEC EDGAR, layer in one cheap paid feed as the account scales. (Pricing verified ~mid-2026; provider pricing changes frequently — re-check before committing.)

**Pre-computed-sentiment news APIs (best starting point):**
- **Alpha Vantage NEWS_SENTIMENT ("Alpha Intelligence")** — *Free: 25 requests/day, 5/min*, end-of-day only; sentiment scores (direction + magnitude) at both article and ticker level are **included on the free tier**. Paid: $49.99/mo (75 req/min, 15-min delayed), $99.99 (150/min, realtime), up to $199.99 (600/min). NASDAQ-licensed, 200k+ tickers, 20+ years price history. Best free on-ramp for AI sentiment scores. Limitation: REST polling only; 25/day is tiny — fine for a small watchlist.
- **Finnhub** — *Free: 60 calls/min* (very generous), no card. Free includes `/company-news` and general news; the dedicated `/news-sentiment` endpoint plus social sentiment (Reddit/Twitter) and insider sentiment (MSPR) are largely **premium-gated**. Cheapest paid ~$50/mo per market (range $11.99–$99.99 by bundle). Up to 30 yrs history on premium. WebSocket support.
- **Marketaux** — *Free: 100 req/day* (3 articles/request); entity-level sentiment (−1 to +1) and `sentiment_avg` aggregates built in. Paid $29/mo (2,500 req/day, 20 articles). 5,000+ sources, 200k+ entities, 80+ markets — good for breadth and international.
- **StockNewsAPI.com** — No permanent free tier (5-day/100-call trial). Basic $19.99/mo (20k calls/mo; sentiment + source/type filters; non-commercial/student); Premium $49.99/mo (50k calls, Top Mentions & Sentiment, Upgrades/Downgrades, historical, commercial). News back to Feb 2019; upgrades/downgrades back to Apr 2022. Sentiment tagged per item (−1.5 to +1.5).
- **Polygon.io / "Massive"** — *Free: 5 calls/min*, EOD/historical. Ticker-news endpoint (`/v3/reference/news`) **includes per-article sentiment + reasoning** (Benzinga-powered). Stocks Starter $29/mo; Benzinga datasets sold as add-ons from $29/mo (not all bundled into Starter). ~25 ms WebSocket news latency on higher tiers — overkill for swing trading but useful for clean tagging.
- **EODHD (EOD Historical Data)** — *Free: 20 calls/day total* but news costs 5 calls/request (~4 news req/day free). News API in the $19.99/mo Calendar & News package (also bundled in $59.99 Fundamentals, $99.99 All-In-One); dedicated **Sentiment Data API** (`/api/sentiments`) returns normalized daily sentiment per ticker plus a News Word Weights API. Good value for a budget build.

**News APIs WITHOUT pre-computed sentiment (you compute it):**
- **Tiingo** — News API is **not on the free tier** (requires the Power tier; exact news add-on price not cleanly published, roughly $10–30/mo). 20M+ articles, ~2 decades, 120+ providers, tagged by ticker/topic — designed for you to run your own NLP. Personal-use license; commercial requires sales contact. Excellent clean historical archive for backtesting.
- **NewsAPI.org** — *Free Developer: 100 req/day, 24-hour delay, 1-month archive, dev/testing ONLY (no production or internal use)*. Cheapest production tier = Business $449/mo (5-yr history). No sentiment, no full article body (headlines/metadata + URL). Poor fit for production trading; fine for prototyping only.

**Analyst-rating / estimate data:** Benzinga (direct, **quote-only pricing**, no public price page; the de-facto analyst-ratings newswire — cheaper indirect access via Polygon add-ons from $29/mo). Finnhub and Financial Modeling Prep (FMP, free tier ~250 req/day) carry analyst estimates and rating data. StockNewsAPI Premium includes an Upgrades/Downgrades feed. Free web sources (Fidelity, Benzinga, MarketBeat) show rating changes but aren't programmatic.

**Free / primary sources (use heavily given small account):**
- **SEC EDGAR** — fully free, no API key (User-Agent header required), **10 req/sec** rate limit. Full-text search API (efts.sec.gov) covers all filings since 2001; filings since 1993 via the data APIs; supports exact-phrase and boolean queries. Pull **8-Ks** (material events: earnings, M&A, guidance, executive/auditor changes, cybersecurity incidents), 10-K/10-Q text for LM-dictionary scoring, Form 4 insider transactions. RSS feeds available. **This is the highest-integrity catalyst feed and it's free** — the backbone of a small-account system.
- **Company IR RSS / press-release wires**, exchange/regulator feeds, **Yahoo Finance** (via the unofficial `yfinance` library — free, no rate limit, but scrapes unofficial endpoints, breaks when Yahoo changes its site, and may violate ToS; fine for experiments, not production).
- **Reddit API** (r/wallstreetbets, r/stocks), **StockTwits API** (its bullish/bearish user tags are a free labeled-sentiment goldmine for training your own classifier), **X/Twitter** (API now expensive/restricted — generally not worth it).

## Scoring methods (NLP for headline & article sentiment)

**Lexicon-based — start here; it's free, fast, transparent, and CPU-only:**
- **Loughran-McDonald (LM) financial dictionary** (Notre Dame SRAF, free) — the canonical finance lexicon. Loughran & McDonald (2011, *Journal of Finance* 66(1):35–65, "When Is a Liability Not a Liability?") showed that **"in a large sample of 10-Ks during 1994 to 2008, almost three-fourths of the words identified as negative by the widely used Harvard Dictionary are words typically not considered negative in financial contexts"** ("liability," "tax," "cost," "depreciation," "vice," "crude"). LM provides word lists: negative, positive, uncertainty, litigious, strong/weak modal, constraining, superfluous. Bag-of-words tone = (pos − neg)/total. This is *the* reason general sentiment tools fail on finance text. Best on formal text (10-Ks, press releases).
- **VADER** — general social-media lexicon; handles negation, intensifiers, emoji, punctuation. Cheap and well-suited to the *register* of Reddit/StockTwits, but it is not finance-aware and underperforms on financial nuance (benchmark studies place it well below FinBERT on financial datasets).

**Transformer / ML — higher accuracy, more cost/latency:**
- **FinBERT** (Araci 2019; also ProsusAI and Yang et al. variants) — BERT further-pretrained on a financial corpus, fine-tuned on the Financial PhraseBank (~110M params). Reported **~86% accuracy on the full Financial PhraseBank and ~97% on the 100%-annotator-agreement subset**; comparative benchmarks show FinBERT F1 ~0.93 / accuracy ~0.91 on SEntFiN and ~0.83–0.85 on FIQA+PhraseBank, beating VADER and general language models by wide margins on financial text. (Some fine-tuned variants like FinancialBERT report even higher PhraseBank scores.) Open-source on Hugging Face; runs on a modest GPU or even CPU for batch jobs. **The pragmatic default** for finance-domain scoring.
- **General LLMs (GPT/Claude)** — flexible, strong at entity-level extraction and context/negation, no training needed (zero/few-shot). Tradeoffs: per-call cost and latency, version drift, and evidence that on *complex* central-bank-style text GPT-4 can underperform FinBERT (over-classifying nuanced sentences as neutral). Best used for entity-level extraction, nuanced longer articles, or batch scoring where latency isn't critical; pin model versions for reproducibility.

**Key design choices:**
- **Finance-domain adaptation is essential** — the "liability/tax" problem above is exactly why you must not run vanilla social sentiment models on financial text.
- **Entity-level vs. document-level** — a headline can be bullish for the acquirer and bearish for a competitor. Score sentiment *toward the specific ticker* (entity-level), not just overall document tone. Marketaux/Alpha Vantage already give per-entity scores; the SEntFiN benchmark is built specifically for entity-level financial sentiment.
- **Negation & context** — VADER handles simple negation; transformers/LLMs handle context far better; lexicon bag-of-words misses "not good."
- **Novelty/staleness weighting** — down-weight recycled stories (Tetlock 2011); RavenPack assigns the first story a novelty score of 100 and decays repeats based on the count of prior stories in the trailing 24-hour window.

## Decay/timing (how news & sentiment decay over days)

- **Daily news sentiment: ~1–2 day predictive horizon** for returns; **weekly-aggregated news: up to one quarter** (Heston & Sinha 2017). Negative news has a long-delayed reaction, much of it concentrated around the next earnings date.
- **Pure media sentiment reverses fast:** Tetlock (2007) — high pessimism predicts next-day downward pressure that reverts within days. García (2013, *Journal of Finance* 68:1267–1300, "Sentiment during Recessions"), using NYT-column word fractions 1905–2005: **"A one standard deviation shock to our news measure during recessions predicts a change in the conditional average return on the DJIA of twelve basis points over one day"** (vs ~3.5 bps in expansions), and the effect **partially reverses over the following four trading days**. Implication: untargeted broad-market "tone" is a weak, mean-reverting, regime-dependent signal — not the basis of a swing system.
- **PEAD / revision drift: weeks to ~60 trading days**, with re-acceleration around the next earnings announcement. Analyst-upgrade "freshness" is short (days) since the rating jump is near-immediate; estimate-revision freshness runs weeks.
- **Practical decay model:** exponential time-decay weight w = exp(−λ·Δt). RavenPack's production approach uses exponential smoothing with tunable decay windows (daily → semi-annual) and finds **negative signals are stronger but decay faster**, and **decay is slower for small/illiquid caps** (slower information incorporation — in APAC backtests, small-cap optimal holding periods ran up to a month vs ~a week for large/mid). For a swing system, a starting calibration of a **news half-life of ~3–5 trading days for general headlines and ~10–20 days for earnings/revision catalysts** is reasonable to then tune on your own data.
- Mechanics: choose a half-life t½, set λ = ln(2)/t½, weight each item's contribution by exp(−λ·days_since).

## Pitfalls (be honest about what's noise)

**Social-media sentiment is mostly noise for *returns*.** The honest academic picture:
- **Antweiler & Frank (2004, *Journal of Finance* 59:1259–1294)** — 1.5M+ Yahoo!/Raging Bull messages: message activity predicts **volatility and trading volume**, but the effect on **returns is "statistically significant but economically small"** (and partly a bid-ask bounce). "Is all that talk just noise?" — largely yes for returns. (Note: their bullishness and "agreement" indices are also mechanically correlated, per Liu et al. 2022 — a caution if you reimplement them.)
- **StockTwits (Divernois & Filipović, 90M messages 2010–2020)** — daily sentiment polarity is **positively associated with *contemporaneous* returns but does not predict next-day returns on average**; predictive power on abnormal returns appears **only conditional on sudden peaks of message volume**. So **volume spikes + polarity** beats polarity alone.
- **Reddit/WSB** — Goyal et al. (2025, arXiv 2508.02089): daily sentiment change alone did *not* significantly correlate with price moves, but a **Sentiment-Volume-Change (SVC)** metric combining sentiment with comment-volume change correlated with next-day returns, especially at extremes. A Lund University thesis found neither more posts nor more positive tone reliably predicted higher company-level returns. Post-GameStop research ("Place Your Bets?") finds WSB DD-report informativeness *deteriorated* after Jan 2021 — **pre-GME recommendations predicted returns; post-GME predictability was eliminated**, with reports shifting toward attention/price-pressure framing. Risk-adjusted profitability of WSB strategies is contested (Buz & de Melo find outperformance; Chacon et al. and Reichenbach & Walther find none after risk adjustment).
- **Twitter "mood":** Bollen et al. (2011) "Twitter mood predicts the stock market" (86.7–87.6% direction accuracy) is **famous but failed to replicate** — Lachanski & Pav (2017) attribute the result to multiple-comparison bias, data snooping, and dropped 2007/early-2008 data, concluding daily Twitter mood is *not* useful for predicting returns. **Do not build on the Bollen result.**

**Pump-and-dump / manipulation (critical for a small account that may trade small caps):**
- Schemes target **low-float micro/penny stocks (OTC/Pink Sheets)** where small buying moves price; promoters coordinate on Telegram/Discord/X, then dump. FINRA warns of both classic pump-and-dump and newer **"ramp-and-dump"** (manipulation via controlled trading, often around small-cap IPOs of foreign issuers).
- Cumming & Tran analyzed 3,800+ small-cap US stocks (2010–2018), ~80M StockTwits posts cross-referenced with 56,000+ NASDAQ SMARTS manipulation alerts, and built a **"noise index"** measuring social activity uncorrelated with fundamentals — a manipulation tell. Cresci et al. (2018) detect coordinated bot groups promoting low-value stocks.
- **Detection/avoidance rules:** exclude or heavily penalize low float + low average dollar volume + low price (<$5); flag sudden mention-volume spikes with no corroborating 8-K or reputable wire; flag coordinated bursts of near-duplicate bullish posts and new/low-karma accounts (bot/astroturf signature); **require a *hard* news corroborator (EDGAR 8-K, major wire) before acting on any social spike.**

**Other pitfalls:** survivorship/snooping bias in any backtest; "sell-the-news" on priced-in events; structural analyst bullish bias; aggregation bias (PEAD looks monotone at the portfolio level but firm-level drifts are noisy — Caltech "Anomalous Anomaly," which found only 51.8% of "good news" firms had positive drift); data-provider ToS/licensing traps (yfinance scraping, NewsAPI dev-only). **The central tension for a small account:** the very drift you want (PEAD, revision drift) is strongest precisely in the illiquid, low-coverage small caps that are hardest and costliest to trade and most prone to manipulation. Manage this with liquidity floors and position sizing, not by chasing the biggest drift numbers.

## Turning this into a computable signal

A robust per-ticker pipeline:

1. **Ingest & dedupe** items (news, filings, posts) tagged to the ticker; drop near-duplicates; assign each a **novelty** flag (first-seen = 1.0, repeats decayed — the RavenPack ENS approach).
2. **Score each item's sentiment** toward the ticker (entity-level): FinBERT for news/headlines, VADER or a FinBERT-tweet variant for social, LM dictionary for filings. Map to [−1, +1].
3. **Weight each item** by wᵢ = (source credibility) × (novelty) × exp(−λ·days_since). Source credibility: major wire / 8-K ≫ aggregator ≫ anonymous social.
4. **Aggregate.** RavenPack research shows **sum-based** aggregation (Sum Excess Sentiment) often beats mean for capturing intensity, while mean controls turnover — track both. Aggregate S = Σ wᵢ·sᵢ (sum) or a weighted mean.
5. **Add a volume/attention sub-signal** — abnormal article/message count vs. baseline. This is where social data earns its keep (StockTwits result: polarity predicts only on volume spikes).
6. **Normalize via z-score** against the ticker's own trailing history (e.g., 60–90 trading days): z = (S − μ)/σ. This handles cross-stock comparability and sparse-news days (no news → revert toward neutral, don't fabricate signal). StockTwits-prediction research explicitly uses z-scores of aggregated sentiment+volume measures.
7. **Map to 0–100** sub-score: e.g., sub = 50 + 50·tanh(z/k) (bounded, monotone), or percentile-rank z against history.
8. **Combine sub-signals into a composite** with explicit weights reflecting evidence strength — overweight earnings-surprise and revision sub-scores, underweight raw social polarity, gate social by volume-spike + hard-news corroboration. Z-score each sub-signal before weighting so no single feed dominates.
9. **Handle sparse days** by decaying the last known score toward neutral 50 rather than holding stale extremes, and require a minimum item count (or a hard-news flag) before the social sub-score is allowed to deviate from 50.

Frameworks worth referencing: RavenPack's Event Sentiment Score (ESS), Composite Sentiment Score (CSS), novelty/ENS decay, and Sum-vs-Mean aggregation studies; Heston-Sinha aggregation-horizon logic; Loughran-McDonald for any filing text; the StockTwits and Reddit-SVC papers for the sentiment×volume interaction.

## Recommendations (staged, with thresholds)

**Stage 1 — free, strongest edge (build first).** Stand up an ingestion pipeline on AWS (Lambda + EventBridge schedule + S3/DynamoDB) pulling: (a) **EDGAR 8-Ks and earnings filings** (free, 10 req/sec), (b) **earnings actuals + consensus** and **estimate revisions** from FMP/Finnhub free tiers, (c) **Alpha Vantage NEWS_SENTIMENT** free tier for pre-computed news sentiment on your watchlist. Compute three sub-scores — SUE/PEAD, revision momentum, headline sentiment — each z-scored against 60–90-day history and mapped to 0–100. Apply a **liquidity floor** (e.g., price ≥ $5 and ≥ $5–10M average daily dollar volume) to sidestep manipulation-prone microcaps. *Benchmark to advance:* an out-of-sample (walk-forward) information coefficient that's positive and stable for the earnings/revision sub-scores over ≥6 months.

**Stage 2 — add FinBERT + one cheap paid feed.** Self-host FinBERT (Hugging Face, runs on a small GPU or CPU batch) to score raw headlines/articles you pull from a cheap feed — **EODHD Calendar & News ($19.99/mo)** or **Marketaux ($29/mo)** — giving you sentiment you control plus volume/attention counts. Add the news-volume-spike sub-signal. *Threshold to add social:* only once Stage-1/2 sub-scores demonstrably add return predictability beyond price-momentum on your own data.

**Stage 3 — social, gated and skeptical.** Add **StockTwits** (free bull/bear tags) and **Reddit** volume-spike detection with bot/astroturf filters, implemented strictly as an **SVC-style sentiment×volume** signal that stays neutral (50) unless a mention spike *and* a hard-news corroborator (8-K/major wire) coincide. Never let social polarity alone trigger a signal.

**What would change these recommendations:** if your walk-forward tests show the earnings/revision sub-scores have *no* edge in tradeable (liquid) names, drop PEAD as a primary driver and lean on revision momentum + price momentum instead. If a paid feed's sentiment doesn't beat free FinBERT on your labeled sample, don't pay for it. If social signals don't survive the volume-spike + corroboration gate out-of-sample, cut them entirely — they cost data-engineering effort and carry manipulation risk.

## Caveats

- **Most "predictive" claims are fragile.** The headline anomalies (PEAD, revision drift) are real but have **decayed materially**, and the social-media results are mixed, in-sample, or non-replicating (Bollen). Treat every number here as a prior to be re-tested on your own out-of-sample data, not a guarantee.
- **The strongest drift lives in the hardest-to-trade names.** Small/illiquid/low-coverage stocks show the biggest PEAD and revision drift but carry the worst slippage and the highest manipulation risk — a structural tension for a small, scaling account.
- **Several data-provider figures are approximate or quote-only.** Benzinga direct pricing is not public; Tiingo's news add-on price isn't cleanly published; Finnhub's lowest paid tier varies by bundle; news-archive depths for Marketaux/EODHD/Polygon aren't fully documented. Verify current pricing and the exact free-tier news-sentiment access before building, as these change often (Alpha Vantage's free tier fell from 500 → 100 → 25 req/day over a few years).
- **Sentiment predicts volume/volatility more reliably than returns.** Antweiler-Frank and the StockTwits work both point this way; if your goal is directional swing signals, weight hard-catalyst sub-signals far above tone.
- **Licensing/ToS and reproducibility:** yfinance and free tiers may not be license-clean for anything beyond personal use; pin LLM model versions; log raw inputs so signals are auditable and backtests are honest.
- This report is for system-design and research purposes and is **not investment advice**; nothing here guarantees profits, and all of these signals can and do fail in live trading.
